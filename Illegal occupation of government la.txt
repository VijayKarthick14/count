

Illegal occupation of government land becomes a big problem and is increasing rapidly. Authorities follow on-ground inspection, that is very time-consuming thus the encroachments of government land keep on increasing. The reason for increasing encroachment is not the lack of effort, it's lack of correct approach using the futuristic tools. Traditional monitoring is performed on paper, physical visits, that generate a report which usually gets delayed and time-consuming. This increases the damage and cost. In rural areas encroachment is increasing rapidly that shows that this method is not working efficiently. By the time the authorities make a report there is already a construction or wall standing on the land. That leads to a long legal battle, and the public loses its construction and affects the land supposed to be used for the public welfare project like a park, school, road, drainage line, and other. Here the real problem is not the public it's the absence of the management system that does not track changes in the government land early. The system must be quick enough to report it at an early stage and keep officials accountable throughout the process.


This project builds a GIS-based Government Land monitoring and encroachments detection platform. This resolves the problem of physical monitoring. This system stores all the Government's land records in a spatial database using PostgreSQL with PostGIS support. Boundaries are saved as geospatial polygons in GeoJSON format and village, taluk, and district boundaries can be digitized using QGIS and loaded directly into the database. Each land unit is linked with its coordinate (latitude and longitude). The backend runs on Python using Flask or Django, handling data processing, alerts, and task windows. The frontend uses React with Leaflet to display an interactive map that is easily navigable by the official and keeps track of the land units.


For satellite images, the platform fetches only the marked land unit in the database using Sentinel-2 through the cloud services, rather than pulling unnecessary datasets. This keeps the system fast and scalable. Encroachment of land is detected by comparing the images of the same land unit over time. OpenCV handles pixel-level analysis to spot new constructions, expansions, or land cover changes. OpenCV detects every pixel of the land unit and checks for any changes such as construction, land boundary, or land cover changes. A CNN model deep dives to classify the features like buildings, roads, and agriculture.


When the change in different timelines of unit land crosses the threshold, it gets flagged. For each flagged encroachment score is recalculated based on how land is affected, how close the land unit is growing and whether the land name, history of encroachment attempt. The moment at which the threshold is crossed, the system auto-drafts a legal notice with land details, detection date, satellite evidence.


The platform also calculates the revenue loss caused by the encroachment that affects or delays the upcoming government schemes on that land. There is also an interface where citizens can raise complaints against suspicious construction through a photo upload. The system cross-checks the complaint with satellite images then, the system provides a timeline view that shows how the land unit has changed month by month using historical imageries, which helps officials to keep reach of only land units. Every action taken to avoid encroachment of government land in the system from detection to notice to resolution is stored completely and permanently into the database.
